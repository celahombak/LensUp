01
Composition · Free
Rule of Thirds
The most fundamental composition rule. Learn to place your subject at the power points of the grid.
● Active
0 / 5 passed
📖 The Lesson

The Rule of Thirds divides your frame into a 3×3 grid — two horizontal lines, two vertical lines. The four points where they cross are called power points.

Place your main subject at one of these four points instead of dead centre. The result feels more natural, more dynamic, and instantly more engaging to the eye.

The same principle applies to horizon lines. Instead of cutting the frame in half, push the horizon to the upper or lower third — whichever part of the scene is most interesting.

Most modern cameras and phones let you enable a thirds grid overlay in settings. Turn it on and practise framing intentionally before you shoot.

4 power points — place your subject on any one
🎯
Main subject — place at any of the 4 intersections, not the centre
🌅
Horizon lines — sky interesting? Horizon on lower third. Ground interesting? Upper third.
👁️
Portraits — place the nearest eye on the upper third line
📱
Tip — enable the grid overlay in your phone camera settings
✓ Rule of Thirds Applied
Subject at left power point, horizon on upper third line
✗ Dead Centre — Avoid This
Subject and horizon both centred — feels static and dull

Leading Lines
Roads, rivers, fences, shadows — use natural lines in your scene to guide the viewer's eye through the frame with purpose.
◎ Free
🔒
Complete Chapter 1 — Rule of Thirds first.
Submit 5 photos scoring 7+ on composition. Each shot teaches you to feel the grid before you learn to break it.
📖 The Lesson

Leading lines are any lines in your scene — roads, fences, rivers, shadows, walls, corridors — that draw the viewer's eye from one part of the frame toward your subject.

The power is in direction and destination. A line that leads nowhere is just a line. A line that carries the eye to your subject is a composition tool.

Lines don't have to be straight. Curves create a slower, more elegant journey through the frame. Diagonal lines add energy and tension. Converging lines — like a road disappearing to a vanishing point — create a powerful sense of depth and distance.

Look for lines before you look for your subject. Then position yourself so the lines serve the story you want to tell.

🛣️
Natural lines — roads, paths, fences, rivers, corridors all work
📐
Diagonal > Horizontal — diagonal lines feel dynamic; flat lines feel static
🎯
Line needs a destination — lead the eye toward your subject, not out of frame
📷
Get low — shooting from ground level exaggerates converging lines dramatically
✓ Leading Lines Applied
Converging fence guides the eye straight to the subject
✗ Line Leads Nowhere
Horizontal fence cuts across frame — doesn't serve the subject

Composition · Paid
Framing
Use doorways, windows, arches and natural openings to frame your subject — adding depth and directing the eye instantly.
🔒 Locked
🔒
Complete Chapter 2 — Leading Lines first.
You need to see your scene as a system of lines before you can use frames within it. Finish Ch2 and this unlocks automatically.
📖 The Lesson

Framing means using elements within the scene — a doorway, a window, an archway, overhanging branches, a tunnel — to create a frame around your subject inside the photo.

It works for three reasons. First, it directs the eye — the natural frame acts like an arrow pointing at your subject. Second, it adds depth by creating foreground, midground and background layers. Third, it gives the photo a sense of place and context that a plain shot of the subject alone cannot.

The frame doesn't have to be sharp. In fact, a slightly soft foreground frame often feels more natural and immersive. What matters is that it doesn't compete with the subject — it should support it.

Look for frames everywhere: a gap between buildings, a cave opening, two trees close together, a person seen through a crowd. Train your eye to see rectangles and circles within your scene.

🏛️
Architecture works great — doorways, windows, arches, tunnels, corridors
🌿
Nature frames too — branches, cave openings, gaps between trees or rocks
🔍
Frame can be out of focus — soft foreground frames feel natural and add depth
⚖️
Frame supports, not competes — if the frame is the subject, it's just a photo of a frame
✓ Framing Applied
Doorway arch creates a natural frame around the subject
✗ No Frame — Subject Floats
Subject competes with background — eye doesn't know where to go
