Chapter 01

Composition

Composition is how you arrange the elements inside your frame. It's the single most powerful tool a photographer has — and unlike lighting or gear, it costs nothing. A well-composed photo guides the viewer's eye exactly where you want it to go.

The Rule of Thirds

Imagine dividing your frame into a 3×3 grid. The four points where the lines intersect are called "power points." Placing your subject on one of these points — rather than dead centre — creates natural tension and visual interest.

Place subjects
on power points
💡 Pro Tip

Most smartphones have a grid overlay built into the camera app. Turn it on in settings — it's the fastest way to train your eye for the rule of thirds.

Leading Lines

Roads, fences, rivers, stairways — any line that draws the eye from the foreground into the background creates depth and guides the viewer through the image. The strongest leading lines point toward your subject.

Framing

Use natural elements like doorways, windows, archways, or tree branches to frame your subject. This creates layers in the image and adds a sense of depth that flat compositions lack.

Negative Space

Leaving empty space around your subject isn't wasted space — it gives the eye room to breathe and makes the subject feel more powerful. Minimalist compositions with strong negative space often score very well.

Chapter 02

Lighting

Photography literally means "writing with light." The quality, direction, and colour of light determines the mood, texture, and drama of your image more than any other single factor.

Golden Hour

The hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset produce warm, soft, directional light that flatters almost any subject. Shadows are long, colours are rich, and the light has a natural glow that's nearly impossible to replicate artificially. If you can only shoot at one time of day, shoot golden hour.

Hard vs Soft Light

Hard light (midday sun, bare flash) creates strong shadows and high contrast — great for dramatic, graphic images. Soft light (overcast sky, window light, diffused flash) creates gentle gradients and flattering skin tones. Neither is better — they serve different stories.

Light Direction

Front lighting is flat and safe. Side lighting (also called Rembrandt lighting) creates shadows that reveal texture and depth. Backlighting creates silhouettes and rim light — dramatic but requires careful exposure. Understanding where your light source is coming from is the first step to controlling it.

💡 Pro Tip

Overcast days are a photographer's best friend for portraits. The entire sky becomes one giant soft box, giving you even, flattering light from everywhere with no harsh shadows.

Exposure Triangle

If you're shooting with a camera or advanced phone settings, three factors control exposure: ISO (sensor sensitivity), Aperture (how much light enters), and Shutter Speed (how long the sensor is exposed). Balancing these three is the foundation of technical photography.

Chapter 03

Colour & Mood

Colour is one of the most emotionally powerful tools in photography. Viewers feel colour before they consciously process it. A deliberate colour palette can make a technically average photo feel unforgettable.

Warm vs Cool Tones

Warm tones (oranges, reds, yellows) feel energetic, intimate, and inviting. Cool tones (blues, greens, purples) feel calm, distant, and melancholic. Most great photos lean intentionally one way or the other rather than sitting neutrally in the middle.

Colour Harmony

Complementary colours (opposite on the colour wheel, like orange and blue) create vibrant contrast. Analogous colours (neighbours on the wheel, like yellow, orange, and red) feel harmonious and natural. Both work — the key is being intentional rather than accidental.

Colour as Story

Think about what story your colours are telling. Desaturated, faded tones feel nostalgic. Rich, vibrant saturation feels bold and modern. Moody, dark tones feel cinematic. Before editing, ask yourself: what feeling do I want this photo to create?

💡 Pro Tip

The most iconic travel photos often have a single dominant colour that ties the whole frame together. Look for scenes where one colour dominates — yellow tuk-tuks, a red door, a blue boat — and simplify around it.

Chapter 04

Focus & Depth of Field

Where you focus in a photo is a creative decision, not just a technical one. What's sharp draws the eye. What's blurred recedes. Controlling this separation is one of the most powerful ways to direct attention.

Shallow Depth of Field

A wide aperture (low f-number like f/1.8 or f/2.8) blurs the background into a smooth wash called "bokeh." This isolates your subject and is the signature look of portrait photography. The subject is sharp; the world melts away behind it.

Deep Depth of Field

A narrow aperture (high f-number like f/11 or f/16) keeps everything sharp from foreground to background. Essential for landscapes, architecture, and street photography where context and environment are part of the story.

Focus Points

Always focus on the most important part of your subject. For portraits, that's the eyes — specifically the eye closest to the camera. A photo where the nose is sharp but eyes are blurry feels fundamentally wrong to every viewer, even if they can't explain why.

💡 Pro Tip

On a smartphone, tap the screen where you want to focus before shooting. Most phones also let you lock focus and exposure separately — look for the AF/AE lock option.

Chapter 05

Understanding Your LensUp Scores

When you analyse a photo with LensUp, Claude AI evaluates it across five dimensions. Here's exactly what each score means and how to improve it.

Score What It Measures How to Improve
Composition How well the frame is arranged — rule of thirds, leading lines, balance, framing Enable grid overlay on your camera app. Slow down before shooting.
Lighting Quality, direction, and exposure of light — is it flattering, dramatic, or harsh? Shoot during golden hour. Use shade to soften harsh midday sun.
Colour Colour palette, harmony, saturation, and whether the tones serve the mood Look for scenes with a dominant colour. Edit with intention, not randomly.
Focus Sharpness of the subject, appropriate depth of field, correct focal point Tap to focus on your subject. For portraits, always focus on the nearest eye.
Mood Emotional impact — does the photo tell a story or evoke a feeling? Ask "what is this photo about?" before pressing the shutter.
📈 Score Benchmarks

1–4: Learning phase — focus on one dimension at a time.  |  5–7: Solid foundations — refine and be more intentional.  |  8–10: Strong work — push creative boundaries.

Chapter 06

Quick Win Tips

These are small changes that make an immediate difference to your scores — no new gear required.

🔲

Turn on the grid

Camera grid overlay trains your eye for composition without thinking.

🚶

Move your feet

Change your angle before zooming. Walking closer or lower changes everything.

🧹

Clean the background

Look behind your subject. Remove distractions before shooting, not after.

⏱️

Wait for the moment

Set up your composition first, then wait for the right light or action.

📏

Get lower

Shooting from eye level is safe. Shooting from knee or ground level is memorable.

🔁

Shoot more frames

Pro photographers take dozens of shots of the same scene. Quantity leads to quality.

✂️

Crop ruthlessly

A tight crop can rescue a weak composition. Remove anything that doesn't add to the story.

🌤️

Embrace bad weather

Fog, rain, and overcast skies create mood that perfect sunny days never can.

Reference

Glossary

Key terms you'll encounter in your LensUp analyses and in photography generally.

Aperture
The opening in a lens that controls how much light reaches the sensor. Measured in f-stops (f/1.8, f/8). Lower number = wider opening = more light + blurrier background.
Bokeh
The aesthetic quality of the out-of-focus blur in a photograph. Creamy, smooth bokeh is highly prized in portrait photography.
Composition
The arrangement of visual elements within the frame. How you position subjects, lines, shapes, and space relative to each other.
Depth of Field
The range of distance in a photo that appears acceptably sharp. Shallow DoF = blurry background. Deep DoF = everything sharp.
Exposure
How bright or dark the final image is. Controlled by ISO, aperture, and shutter speed together — the "exposure triangle."
Golden Hour
The period shortly after sunrise and before sunset when sunlight is warm, soft, and directional. Considered the best natural light for photography.
ISO
The sensitivity of the camera sensor to light. Higher ISO brightens images in dark conditions but introduces grain (noise).
Leading Lines
Lines within the frame (roads, fences, rivers) that guide the viewer's eye toward the main subject or through the image.
Negative Space
Empty or uncluttered areas around the subject. Gives the eye room to breathe and makes subjects feel more powerful and isolated.
Rule of Thirds
A compositional guideline that divides the frame into a 3×3 grid. Placing subjects at the intersections creates natural visual balance.
Shutter Speed
How long the camera sensor is exposed to light. Fast speeds freeze motion; slow speeds create motion blur and light trails.
White Balance
A camera setting that adjusts colours so whites appear neutral under different light sources (daylight, tungsten, fluorescent).
